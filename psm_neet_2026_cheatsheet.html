📐 Study Designs — Hierarchy of Evidence
Systematic Review/Meta-analysis → RCT → Cohort → Case-Control → Cross-sectional → Case Report → Expert Opinion
RCTGold standard for causation; randomisation eliminates confounding
Cohort studyExposure → Outcome; calculates RR; can study incidence; prospective or retrospective
Case-control studyOutcome → Exposure (backwards); calculates OR; best for rare diseases
Cross-sectionalPrevalence study; exposure + outcome measured simultaneously
Ecological studyGroup-level data; ecological fallacy risk
Best for rare diseaseCase-control study
Best for rare exposureCohort study
Fastest & cheapestCross-sectional study
📊 Measures of Association
2×2 Table: Disease+ Disease- Exposed: a b Unexposed: c d RR = [a/(a+b)] ÷ [c/(c+d)] OR = (a×d) / (b×c) AR = Risk(exposed) − Risk(unexposed) PAR% = (Incidence total − Incidence unexposed) / Incidence total × 100
RR = 1No association
RR > 1Positive association (risk factor)
RR < 1Negative association (protective)
OR approximates RR whenDisease is rare (<10% prevalence)
NNT1 / ARR; number needed to treat to prevent 1 event
NNH1 / AR; number needed to harm 1 person
⚠️ Bias Types — Exam Favourites
BiasDefinition / Example
Selection biasNon-random selection; Berkson's bias (hospital-based case-control); healthy worker effect
Information biasSystematic error in measurement; recall bias (case-control); interviewer bias
Recall biasCases remember exposure better than controls; case-control studies
Lead time biasScreening detects disease earlier → appears to survive longer without actual benefit
Length biasScreening preferentially picks slowly progressive disease
Hawthorne effectPeople change behaviour because they know they are being observed
Neyman biasPrevalence-incidence bias; mild/fatal cases excluded from cross-sectional study
Berkson's biasHospital-based controls differ from general population; case-control specific
ConfoundingThird variable associated with both exposure and outcome; not a bias per se
Randomisation is the best method to control confounding in RCTs. Matching and stratification used in observational studies.
🔬 Causation — Bradford Hill Criteria
Mnemonic: STAMPED BC Strength, Temporality (only necessary one), Analogy, M-Plausibility, Plausibility (biological), Experiment, Dose-response, Biological gradient, Consistency, Coherence
Only essential criterionTemporality — cause must precede effect
Strongest single criterionStrength of association (high RR)
Koch's postulatesOrganism present in every case; cultivable; reproduces disease; reisolatable
Evans' criteriaModified Koch's for chronic diseases and serological evidence
Sufficient causeAlways produces disease; not always present
Necessary causeMust be present; disease cannot occur without it

Propagated sourceMultiple peaks; person-to-person spread; each peak one incubation period apart
Attack rateNumber ill / Number exposed × 100 (used in outbreak)
Secondary attack rateNew cases in household contacts / susceptible household contacts × 100

🎯 Sensitivity & Specificity
Disease+ Disease- Test +: TP FP Test -: FN TN Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN) → "True Positive Rate" Specificity = TN / (TN + FP) → "True Negative Rate" PPV = TP / (TP + FP) NPV = TN / (TN + FN) Accuracy = (TP + TN) / Total
High sensitivity test used forScreening (SnNout — Sensitive test, Negative rules OUT)
High specificity test used forConfirmation (SpPin — Specific test, Positive rules IN)
PPV depends onPrevalence — ↑ prevalence → ↑ PPV
NPV depends onPrevalence — ↑ prevalence → ↓ NPV
Sensitivity & SpecificityIntrinsic properties — NOT affected by prevalence
Likelihood ratio +veSensitivity / (1 − Specificity); >10 = strong evidence

Survival data / time-to-eventKaplan-Meier; Log-rank test; Cox regression
Adjust for multiple confounders, continuous outcomeMultiple linear regression
Adjust for multiple confounders, binary outcomeLogistic regression → gives adjusted OR
📈 Distributions & Normal Curve
Normal distributionMean = Median = Mode; bell-shaped; symmetrical
Mean ± 1 SD68.2% of observations
Mean ± 2 SD95.4% of observations
Mean ± 3 SD99.7% of observations
95% CIMean ± 1.96 × SE; if CI crosses 1 (for OR/RR) → not significant
Positive skewTail to right; Mean > Median > Mode (MMM — Mode Most Medial)
Negative skewTail to left; Mean < Median < Mode
SE of meanSD / √n; decreases with increasing sample size
Type I error (α)False positive; reject true null hypothesis; p-value
Type II error (β)False negative; fail to reject false null hypothesis
Power of study1 − β; probability of detecting true effect; want ≥80%
p-value <0.05Statistically significant (5% level); reject H0
🔬 ROC Curve & Cut-off Points
ROC curve axesY-axis: Sensitivity; X-axis: 1 − Specificity (FPR)
AUC = 0.5Diagonal line; no discriminatory ability (chance)
AUC = 1.0Perfect test; 100% sensitivity and specificity
AUC >0.7Acceptable; >0.8 = good; >0.9 = excellent
Shift cut-off to LEFT↑ Sensitivity, ↓ Specificity; more FP; used for screening
Shift cut-off to RIGHT↑ Specificity, ↓ Sensitivity; more FN; used for confirmation
Best cut-off point on ROCTop-left corner (maximises Se + Sp); Youden's index
Youden's indexSensitivity + Specificity − 1; maximum = best cut-off

🔍 Screening — Wilson & Jungner Criteria
Mnemonic: STOP IT S — Suitable test (safe, simple, acceptable, low cost) T — Treatment available O — Opinions agreed on diagnosis & treatment P — Prevalence high enough to justify screening I — Important health problem T — Treatable (early treatment better than late)
Ideal screening test hasHigh sensitivity (to not miss cases)
Confirmatory test hasHigh specificity (to confirm true disease)
Lead time biasScreening appears to prolong survival but only advances diagnosis date
Mass screeningEntire population
Selective screeningHigh-risk groups only (more efficient)
Multiphasic screeningMultiple tests simultaneously
Serial testing↑ Specificity; used for confirmation
Parallel testing↑ Sensitivity; used for screening (either test positive = positive)
