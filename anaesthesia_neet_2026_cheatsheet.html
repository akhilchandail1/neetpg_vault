🌡️ Stages of Anaesthesia (Guedel)
Stage I — AnalgesiaConscious; amnesia begins; pain reduced; eye movements — voluntary
Stage II — Excitement/DeliriumUnconscious; breath-holding; vomiting; laryngospasm risk; irregular breathing; no surgery here
Stage III Plane 1Eyelid reflex lost; regular breathing; eyes start to deviate
Stage III Plane 2Surgical anaesthesia; regular breathing; corneal reflex lost; eyes central and fixed
Stage III Plane 3Intercostal paralysis begins; diaphragmatic breathing; pupil dilates
Stage III Plane 4Complete intercostal paralysis; diaphragmatic only; deep anaesthesia
Stage IV — Medullary depressionApnoea; circulatory failure; death if uncorrected
Signs of adequate depthFixed central pupils; regular breathing; haemodynamic stability; no lacrimation/sweating
📊 MAC (Minimum Alveolar Concentration)
MAC definitionAlveolar concentration preventing movement in 50% of patients to surgical skin incision
MAC — additive propertyMACs of different agents are additive (0.5 MAC + 0.5 MAC = 1 MAC)
Factors ↑ MAC (need more drug)Hyperthermia, hyperthyroidism, chronic alcoholism, hypernatraemia, young age, anxiety, red hair
Factors ↓ MAC (need less drug)Hypothermia, hypothyroidism, acute alcohol, opioids, pregnancy, elderly, anaemia, hypoxia, hypotension
MAC-awake~0.3–0.4 MAC; loss of consciousness
MAC-BAR (block adrenergic response)~1.5 MAC; suppresses autonomic response to incision
Meyer-Overton rulePotency correlates with oil:gas partition coefficient (lipid solubility)
📡 Intraoperative Monitoring
Minimum monitoring (ASA standard)ECG + SpO₂ + NIBP + EtCO₂ + temperature
Gold standard for ETT placementCapnography (EtCO₂ waveform)
Normal EtCO₂35–45 mmHg (PaCO₂ is ~5 mmHg higher)
EtCO₂ sudden ↓ to zeroOesophageal intubation / circuit disconnection / cardiac arrest
EtCO₂ sudden ↑MH (malignant hyperthermia — earliest sign), ↑metabolism, hypoventilation, CO₂ absorption (lap surgery)
BIS (Bispectral Index)EEG-derived; depth of anaesthesia; 40–60 = surgical anaesthesia; >80 = awake
Pulse oximetry — limitationInaccurate in: metHb (reads ~85%), COHb (falsely high), nail polish, peripheral vasoconstriction
Awareness under anaesthesia0.1–0.2% overall; highest risk: cardiac surgery, caesarean section, trauma

⚠️ Difficult Airway Algorithm
Cannot intubate — can ventilateLMA / facemask → call for help → videolaryngoscopy → surgical airway
Cannot intubate — cannot ventilate (CICV)Emergency surgical airway immediately (cricothyroidotomy / tracheostomy)
CICV — immediate actionNeedle cricothyroidotomy (temporising) → surgical cricothyroidotomy
Videolaryngoscope — advantageBetter glottic view; anterior larynx; C-MAC, McGrath, GlideScope
Awake fibreoptic intubationGold standard for anticipated difficult airway; patient cooperative; topical anaesthesia
Retrograde intubationWire passed retrograde through cricothyroid membrane → guided intubation
Failed intubation drillCall for help → optimise position → 2 further attempts → LMA → surgical airway
Extubation criteriaAwake + responsive + sustained head lift >5 sec + Vt >5 mL/kg + SpO₂ >95% on FiO₂ 0.3
🚨 Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI)
IndicationsFull stomach, GERD, pregnancy (>14 wks), trauma, bowel obstruction, emergency surgery
Preoxygenation100% O₂ for 3–5 min (denitrogenation); SpO₂ maintained during apnoea
Induction agent in RSIThiopentone (traditional) / Propofol / Ketamine (haemodynamic instability)
Muscle relaxant in RSISuccinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg (fastest onset) OR Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg (if succinylcholine CI)
Cricoid pressure (Sellick's manoeuvre)10 N (awake) → 30 N (unconscious); compresses oesophagus; controversial (may worsen view)
No bag-mask ventilationAvoids gastric insufflation and regurgitation risk
Rocuronium reversal in RSISugammadex 16 mg/kg (immediately reverses 1.2 mg/kg rocuronium)

🌬️ Nitrous Oxide — Special Points
MAC of N₂O105% (cannot produce anaesthesia alone at 1 atm)
Second gas effectN₂O uptake concentrates co-administered agent (e.g. halothane) → faster induction
Concentration effectHigher inspired concentration → faster rise of alveolar concentration
Diffusion hypoxia (Fink effect)Post-N₂O: rapid N₂O diffusion out → dilutes alveolar O₂; give 100% O₂ for 5–10 min at end
Expands closed gas spacesAvoid in: pneumothorax, bowel obstruction, middle ear surgery, air embolism, vitreous gas (eye surgery), intracranial air
Mechanism of actionNMDA receptor antagonism (analgesic); also GABA modulation
Bone marrow suppressionOxidises vitamin B12 → inactivates methionine synthase → megaloblastic anaemia (prolonged exposure)
Entonox50% N₂O + 50% O₂; labour analgesia; procedure sedation; pseudo-critical temperature −7°C

Mapleson A (Magill)Most efficient for spontaneous ventilation; FGF = minute ventilation; rebreathing-free

🫀 Cardiovascular Effects of Inhalational Agents
All volatile agents↓SVR (vasodilation); ↓myocardial contractility (dose-dependent)
Halothane — unique CV effectSensitises myocardium to catecholamines → ventricular arrhythmias (avoid epinephrine with halothane)
Isoflurane — CV↑HR (reflex); ↓SVR; ↓BP; coronary vasodilation
Desflurane — CVSympathetic activation with rapid ↑concentration → ↑HR + ↑BP transiently
Sevoflurane — CVMildest CV depression; QT prolongation possible
N₂O — CV effect↑Sympathetic tone (↑NE release); mild myocardial depression; ↑PVR (avoid in pulmonary HTN)

🧠 Local Anaesthetic — Mechanisms
MOAReversible block of Na⁺ channels (inner surface); block in use-dependent manner
Unionised form (B)Crosses membrane (lipophilic); pKa determines ratio
Ionised form (BH⁺)Binds Na⁺ channel from inside; active blocking form
Alkaline pH → more unionisedFaster onset; sodium bicarbonate speeds onset
Acidic pH (infected tissue)More ionised → poor penetration → LA fails in abscess
Adrenaline added to LA↓Systemic absorption; ↑duration; ↓bleeding; marker of IV injection (tachycardia)
Adrenaline CI with LAEnd-arteries (finger, toe, nose, ear, penis) — ischaemic necrosis
Nerve fibre sensitivity orderB > C > Aδ > Aγ > Aβ > Aα (smallest unmyelinated fastest blocked)
Differential blockPain/temperature lost first → touch → proprioception → motor (last)

⚡ Neuromuscular Monitoring (TOF)
Train-of-Four (TOF) stimulation4 supramaximal stimuli at 2 Hz; count twitches; TOF ratio = T4/T1
TOF ratio >0.9Adequate neuromuscular recovery for extubation
TOF count 4 (fade)~75% receptor blockade (light block)
TOF count 3~80% receptor blockade
TOF count 1–2~90–95% receptor blockade
TOF count 0 (no twitches)>95% blockade; deep block; PTC used
Double burst stimulation (DBS)Detects residual blockade better than TOF tactilely
Post-tetanic count (PTC)Used during intense/deep blockade when TOF = 0
Depolarising block (Phase I) vs Phase IIPhase I: no fade, no post-tetanic facilitation; Phase II: fade present, post-tetanic facilitation present (mimics NDMR)

⚠️ Succinylcholine — Contraindications & Special
Absolute CI — hyperkalaemia riskBurns (>24 hrs onset, up to 2 yrs); crush injury; upper/lower motor neuron injury; prolonged immobility; severe intra-abdominal infection
Safe window post-traumaFirst 24 hrs after injury (safe); becomes dangerous 24 hrs – 2 years
Pseudocholinesterase deficiencyProlonged apnoea ("scoline apnoea"); dibucaine number ↓; fresh frozen plasma / wait for metabolism
Dibucaine number — normal80 (80% inhibition); heterozygous = 60; homozygous abnormal = 20
MH triggerSuccinylcholine + all volatile agents (except N₂O); treat with dantrolene
FasciculationsPrevented by pre-curarisation: small dose NDMR (e.g. vecuronium 0.01 mg/kg) 3 min before
Bradycardia with suxamethoniumChildren > adults; prevented by atropine; especially with repeated doses

🔥 Malignant Hyperthermia (MH)
MechanismAutosomal dominant; RYR1 mutation; uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from SR → hypermetabolic crisis
TriggersAll volatile agents + Succinylcholine (NOT N₂O, propofol, NDMR, opioids, benzodiazepines)
Earliest sign↑EtCO₂ (↑CO₂ production — unexpected rise despite unchanged ventilation)
Classic triad↑EtCO₂ + Tachycardia + Hyperthermia (temperature rises 1–2°C every 5 minutes)
Other signsMasseter spasm (triggered by suxamethonium), metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, ↑CK, ↑K⁺, myoglobinuria, DIC
Immediate managementStop trigger agent; 100% O₂ hyperventilate; call for help; get dantrolene
Specific treatmentDantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV bolus; repeat up to 10 mg/kg total; inhibits Ca²⁺ release from SR
Dantrolene continued1–2 mg/kg every 6 hrs for 24–48 hrs (prevent recurrence)
Confirmatory testCaffeine-halothane contracture test (CHCT) on muscle biopsy (Leeds/Toronto)
Safe agents in MH-susceptiblePropofol, N₂O, NDMR, opioids, benzodiazepines, LA — all safe
🚨 Anaphylaxis Under Anaesthesia
MC trigger in anaesthesiaNeuromuscular blocking drugs (NMBDs) — especially rocuronium; also latex, antibiotics
Clinical featuresBronchospasm + hypotension + rash (urticaria/flushing) + angioedema; may lack skin signs in GA
Immediate treatmentStop trigger; 100% O₂; IV adrenaline 1:10,000 (0.1 mg = 1 mL); call for help
Adrenaline dose (IV — anaesthesia)50–100 µg IV boluses (1:10,000); titrate; repeat every 1–2 min; adrenaline infusion if refractory
Secondary treatmentChlorphenamine + hydrocortisone + IV fluid; salbutamol for bronchospasm
Serum tryptaseMast cell marker; take 1–2 hrs and 24 hrs post-event; helps confirm anaphylaxis
Latex allergy — at-risk patientsSpina bifida (MC), healthcare workers, multiple surgeries, rubber industry workers
Latex cross-reactivityAvocado, banana, kiwi, chestnut (latex-fruit syndrome)

🔥 Exam Traps & Frequently Confused
Etomidate — single dose in sepsisAVOID — single induction dose causes adrenal suppression for 24 hrs; relative adrenal insufficiency
Ketamine — avoid in head injury↑ICP (↑CMRO₂ + ↑CBF); traditionally avoided — now evidence suggests safe with ventilation
Bupivacaine 0.75% — obstetricsBANNED for obstetric epidural (cardiac arrest on accidental IV); max 0.5% for epidural
Neostigmine without anticholinergicNever give alone — causes bradycardia, bronchospasm, ↑secretions (always pair with atropine/glycopyrrolate)
N₂O in middle ear surgeryExpands air spaces — ↑pressure in middle ear → tympanic membrane rupture / graft displacement
Depolarising vs non-depolarising blockDepolarising (Phase I): no fade, no post-tetanic facilitation, ↑by anticholinesterase | Non-depolarising: fade present, reversed by neostigmine
Halothane hepatitisType I (mild, common, reversible) vs Type II (immune-mediated, rare, fatal hepatic necrosis — repeat exposure)
Phenylephrine vs ephedrine choicePhenylephrine = alpha agonist (better fetal pH); Ephedrine = alpha+beta (use if bradycardia with hypotension)
