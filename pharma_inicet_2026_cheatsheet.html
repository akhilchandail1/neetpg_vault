Pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, drug interactions — core concepts
📐 Pharmacokinetic Parameters
Bioavailability (F)Fraction of drug reaching systemic circulation unchanged
IV bioavailability100% (by definition)
Volume of distribution (Vd)Dose / plasma concentration (apparent volume)
Vd <1 L/kgConfined to plasma/ECF (heparin, warfarin)
Vd >1 L/kgWidely distributed (chloroquine, digoxin, amiodarone)
Clearance (CL)Volume of plasma cleared per unit time
Half-life (t½)0.693 × Vd / CL
Steady state achieved~4–5 half-lives
Loading doseVd × target Cp (used when rapid effect needed)
Therapeutic index (TI)TD50 / ED50 (narrow TI = dangerous: digoxin, lithium, warfarin, phenytoin, aminoglycosides)
Narrow TI drugs (DLAW-P): Digoxin, Lithium, Aminoglycosides, Warfarin, Phenytoin
⚗️ Zero vs First Order Kinetics
First-order kineticsFixed fraction eliminated per unit time (most drugs)
Zero-order kineticsFixed amount eliminated per unit time (saturable)
Zero-order drugsPhenytoin, Ethanol, Aspirin (high dose), Heparin, Warfarin (at high conc)
Phenytoin kineticsZero-order at therapeutic doses (Michaelis-Menten)
t½ in zero-orderNOT constant — increases as dose increases
t½ in first-orderConstant regardless of dose
Michaelis-MentenLow dose = 1st order; High dose = 0th order
Small dose change of phenytoin causes disproportionate plasma level rise — monitor TDM
🔄 First-Pass Metabolism
DefinitionPresystemic hepatic metabolism before reaching circulation
High first-pass drugsMorphine, Propranolol, Lignocaine, Nitrates (GTN), Labetalol, Verapamil, Salbutamol (oral), Testosterone
Sublingual route bypassesFirst-pass (GTN, buprenorphine, ergotamine)
Transdermal bypassesFirst-pass (fentanyl, nicotine, GTN patch)
Rectal routePartially bypasses first-pass (~50%)
Prodrug advantageAvoids first-pass; activated after absorption
Prodrug examplesEnalapril→Enalaprilat; Codeine→Morphine; Levodopa→Dopamine
🧬 Drug Metabolism & Enzymes
Phase I reactionsOxidation, Reduction, Hydrolysis (CYP450 mainly)
Phase II reactionsConjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation, methylation)
CYP3A4Metabolises ~50% of drugs; intestinal + hepatic
CYP2D6Codeine, TCAs, β-blockers; poor metabolisers: Caucasians 7%
CYP450 inducersRIPES: Rifampicin, Isoniazid (chronic), Phenytoin/Phenobarbitone, Ethanol (chronic), Smoking/St John's Wort/Carbamazepine/Griseofulvin
CYP450 inhibitorsSICKFACES: Sulfonamides, Isoniazid (acute), Cimetidine, Ketoconazole, Fluconazole, Amiodarone, Ciprofloxacin, Erythromycin, SSRIs
Grapefruit juiceInhibits CYP3A4 (intestinal) → ↑drug levels (statins, CCBs, cyclosporine)
Slow acetylators↑risk INH toxicity (neuropathy, hepatotoxicity); Hydralazine SLE
RIPES = Inducers | SICKFACES = Inhibitors
📊 Pharmacodynamics
AgonistBinds receptor + activates (full/partial)
Full agonistMaximum efficacy (Emax); e.g. morphine
Partial agonistSubmaximal effect even with 100% receptor occupancy (buprenorphine)
AntagonistBinds but NO activation; blocks agonist
Competitive antagonist↑Km (↑EC50), same Emax; reversible by ↑agonist
Non-competitive antagonist↓Emax; cannot overcome; irreversible or allosteric
Inverse agonistOpposite effect to agonist (e.g. β-carboline at BZD receptor)
PotencyEC50 (dose to get 50% effect) — lower EC50 = more potent
EfficacyMaximum effect achievable (Emax)
TachyphylaxisRapid tolerance on repeated dosing (ephedrine, nitrates)
🧪 Plasma Protein Binding
Highly protein bound drugsWarfarin 99%, Diazepam 98%, Phenytoin 90%, Furosemide 99%, NSAIDs 95%+
Clinical significanceOnly free drug is active; interactions when displaced
Acidic drugs bindAlbumin (warfarin, NSAIDs, furosemide)
Basic drugs bindα1-acid glycoprotein (propranolol, lignocaine, TCAs)
Renal failure effect↓albumin → ↑free drug → enhanced effects/toxicity
Warfarin + aspirin interactionDisplacement → ↑free warfarin → bleeding risk
Drug not dialysable ifHigh Vd OR high protein binding

ConceptDetailExample
Glomerular filtrationFree (unbound) drug filtered; size <7 nmInulin (marker), small drugs
Tubular secretionActive transport; organic acid (OAT) or base (OCT) transportersPenicillin, uric acid, metformin
Tubular reabsorptionPassive; lipophilic/uncharged drugs reabsorbedEthanol, lipid-soluble drugs
